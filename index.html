My Name is Vaibhava

A first-person account. I tell how I found myself, how I fell for Terry Liang, and how my life opened like color.
First person, honest voice

Introduction

I am writing this in the small hours. The city lights are tired and my tea has gone lukewarm. I write because a life held quiet becomes small. I write because I want someone like me to find this page and not feel alone. I am gay. I like men. I fell in love with Terry Liang.

That feels both simple and enormous. I do not want a drama. I only want truth. This is my story in scenes, in small entries, in the things I carried and the moments that made me who I am.

Childhood
Ages 0–12

Childhood

I loved color first. My mother gave me crayons and I kept the pink for myself. My father liked practical things, but he smiled when I made paper animals. I collected stickers and kept them under my pillow. When other boys played rough games, I sat with scissors and glue and wonder.

I remember hiding a bright scarf in my backpack because I liked the way it moved. I remember thinking the world would be kinder if people could say what they truly felt. I did not know then how to say what I felt.

Age 8

Age 11

School
Ages 12–18

School Years

At school, I learned how to hide. Teasing was a lesson. I learned to laugh when others did and to shrink when it mattered. Still, there were glances that lasted longer than they should. A boy's smile in the corridor could set my chest a little off balance.

I found poets in the library who wrote about strange loves and small freedoms. Their lines felt like windows. I read them with the lights off, holding a flashlight to the page as if the words were secret friends.

College
Early twenties

College and Discovery

College was the first time I felt the air change. I met people who named things I had not yet put words to. I joined a zine group and wrote small pieces about color and place. I felt seen by small groups and that made me brave enough to notice my feelings more clearly.

I remember a night in the student common room when I realized I could say the words out loud. I said them to myself first, then to a friend: I like men. The friend nodded like it was the plainer thing in the world. That nod felt like permission.

Terry
The crush that changed everything

Terry Liang

Terry arrived in my life like a tune I couldn't get out of my head. He was quiet in a way that felt like room to breathe. He carried a thermos and a stack of music cassettes. He laughed with his whole mouth. He had a scar on his knuckle from fixing a bike and he told stories like he was making them up as he went.

I first noticed him in class when he corrected a misquote and then turned the conversation to an old singer I loved. He looked at me when I spoke and I felt seen. Small things mattered: the way he wore his coat, the way he tapped his foot while listening to records. I began to wait for him.

How it began

I asked to borrow a cassette. He said yes and pressed a mixtape into my hands. On my way home I held it like it was fragile treasure. I met him at the library a week later and he slid a note across the table: 'Do you want to hear more sometime?'

That note was the start. We walked home under a late sky. We talked about silly things and then not-silly things. He told me about his grandmother's recipes, I told him about the ribbon I kept. He listened. When he listened I grew braver.

First gentle moments

We studied together in the art room. I spilled tea on a sketch and he laughed in a way that made me show the ruined page. He drew a repair: a tiny painted strip that made the spill look like part of the work. He reached across the table to point out a color and our fingers brushed. It was the kind of touch that is both startling and ordinary. I felt the world tilt toward possibility.

"When Terry smiled, I forgot the sentences I meant to say. I learned new sentences instead."

A quiet confession

One evening, after a small show of prints, we walked by the river. The city hummed behind us. I told him I liked him. I worried it would crease us. He looked at me, thoughtful, then took my hand. He whispered that he'd been waiting for me to say it. I remember the steadiness of his palm. Saying the words made something uncurl inside me.

Coming Out
Family and friends

Coming Out

Telling my parents felt like a tide. I wrote a letter at first so the words would not evaporate. I put it on the kitchen table and waited. My mother read, then reached for me. My father asked questions that showed he wanted to understand. They did not always say the right thing, but they said they loved me.

Terry met them months later. He brought a small bouquet of wildflowers. My mother cried a little and then handed me a cup of tea like she did when I was small. After that, I began to measure time by the simple ways people show care.

Now
Work, art, and chosen family

Now

We live together in a small apartment with a balcony full of plants. Terry makes breakfast on Saturdays and I make playlists. We argue about the best way to fold laundry and then apologize with coffee. We have a ritual of writing one small note to each other every week. Mine say things like: You made the plants taller. You make color louder.

We go to Pride and we hold hands. We host friends and we listen to their stories. Some of our friends struggle. We show up. I work with a community center and Terry teaches music. We both find ways to give back to a younger version of ourselves.

Small joys

  • The way he hums while washing dishes.
  • The mixtapes he makes with songs that remind him of summer markets.
  • The quiet mornings when we read on the couch and the cat falls asleep on our feet.
